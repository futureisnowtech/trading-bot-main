## TASK 3: Article Critique (For This Specific Bot)

### What the Article Gets Right

1. **Fee-first thinking is correct.** The article's emphasis on fees as the primary edge-killer applies here. At $250 position size and ~0.8% round-trip fees on Coinbase, the break-even move is ~2.4% gross. The ATR fee-floor guard directly addresses this.

2. **Signal confluence is right.** The article's multi-signal approach matches this bot's architecture. Single-signal entries are inherently noisy; requiring conviction score across 19 signals is better.

3. **Paper-first is correct.** This bot has a proper paper phase. The article's incubation concept is sound.

4. **Benchmark thresholds are useful guidance.** WR > 55%, PF > 1.5 are good targets even if the bot's current thresholds are lower.

### Where the Article Fails For This Environment

1. **Market assumption mismatch.** The article is written for Polymarket (prediction markets — binary outcomes, no position sizing, no fees on most events). This bot trades Coinbase crypto (order-book-based, continuous pricing, maker/taker fees, slippage, partial fills). The article's mechanics don't translate directly.

2. **Limit order preference.** The article recommends limit orders to reduce fees. On Coinbase Advanced Trade, maker fees are 0.4% vs taker 0.6%. For a $250 position, that's $1.00 vs $1.50 per side — a $0.50 difference. For a 30-trade incubation period, that's $15 total. Meaningful but not a strategic priority at this account size. More importantly, limit orders introduce execution uncertainty (fill risk) that could cost more than the fee savings in missed entries.

3. **Win rate benchmark is wrong for AI-debate systems.** A system that relies on 8+ AI agents to vote on every trade will inherently reject many borderline cases. The actual WR target should be calibrated to THIS system's R:R ratio. With 3:1 R:R (1.5% stop, 4.5% target), break-even WR is 25%. The article's 55% target assumes 1:1 R:R common in prediction markets.

   Break-even WR formula: BE_WR = R_loss / (R_win + R_loss) = 1.5 / (4.5 + 1.5) = **25%**

   A 35% WR with 3:1 R:R yields positive expectancy. The article's 55% benchmark is too high for this system.

4. **Sample size is wrong.** The article requires 100+ trades before graduating a strategy. This bot has 20 crypto pairs × multiple signals. With 1-2 trades/day average, gathering 100 trades takes 50-100 days. That's appropriate for equity but aggressive for a crypto bot that has 4,857 simulated trades already seeded.

5. **Walk-forward is more important here than the article implies.** Crypto regimes shift quickly (bull→bear→sideways in weeks). A single 30-day backtest can show excellent results during a trending regime then fail catastrophically when the regime shifts. The article doesn't address this adequately.

6. **Backtest-to-live gap.** The article assumes backtest and live execution are the same. In this system, they're fundamentally different: backtest uses math-only signals, live uses those signals plus an 8-agent AI debate. The AI panel can veto entries that the backtest would have taken. This means live performance should be BETTER than backtest (more filtering), but it also means backtest results don't directly validate the live system.

## TASK 5: Revised Framework — RBIPMS

The article's 3-phase RBI framework is correct in spirit but incomplete for a live autonomous trading system. This bot needs 6 phases:

```
R → B → I → P → M → S/R
│    │   │   │   │    │
│    │   │   │   │    └─ Scale / Retire
│    │   │   │   └────── Monitor (live performance tracking)
│    │   │   └────────── Promote (paper→live graduation)
│    │   └────────────── Incubate (paper trading)
│    └────────────────── Backtest (historical + rolling validation)
└─────────────────────── Research (hypothesis + signal design)
```

### Phase definitions:

**R — Research**: Signal hypothesis documented, entry/exit math defined, fee model included, regime scope specified.

**B — Backtest**: Walk-forward OOS validation (not just single-period). Must pass: WR ≥ 35% (adjusted for 3:1 R:R), PF ≥ 1.3, Sharpe ≥ 0.5, max_dd ≤ 20%, ≥ 30 OOS trades. Backtest must use same fee model as live.

**I — Incubate**: Minimum 14 calendar days paper trading (no fast-track). Position sizing: 50% of live size during incubation. Daily performance review vs backtest projections. Circuit breaker: 3 consecutive losing days → extend incubation 7 days.

**P — Promote**: Formal graduation checklist (see `02_incubation_playbook.md`). All 7 check_readiness criteria + 3 additional: live WR ≥ backtest WR - 10pp, no > 2 consecutive losing days in last 5, agent agreement ≥ 50% on winning trades.

**M — Monitor**: Monthly performance review against backtest benchmarks. Rolling 30-day WR tracked. Regime drift detection (is the strategy still trading in its target regime?). Auto-demotion trigger if performance degrades below 60% of backtest benchmark for 2 consecutive weeks.

**S/R — Scale or Retire**: Scale: position size increase in 25% increments, one increment per 30 profitable days. Retire: formal retirement criteria (see `03_strategy_lifecycle.md`). Retirement is not a failure — it's evidence-based strategy management.

### The Critical Risk No One Is Talking About

**The backtest-to-live gap is the most dangerous unknown in this system.** The backtest engine runs math signals only. The live system runs math signals + 8 AI agents + Goku veto + session analyst + macro gate + prescreener. These are fundamentally different decision processes.

The 4,857 seeded trades are from the **backtest path** (math-only). The Bayesian weights they populated reflect the math-signal edge, not the AI-debate edge. Every time the AI panel overrides a math signal (which is frequent), the attribution data diverges from what the weights were trained on.

**This is acceptable for now** — the live attribution data will progressively replace the seeded data as real trades accumulate. But it means:
1. The system's early live performance could diverge significantly from backtest projections
2. The rolling backtest validator (live_backtest_validator.py) is validating the math-only path, not the live path
3. There is no way to know the live system's true expected WR until ~30 real trades are completed

**Recommendation:** Track live WR vs seeded WR explicitly. If live WR diverges more than 15pp from seeded WR after 30 trades, run a root-cause review before scaling position sizes.
